Solution: Once again the trick is to express \(\frac{1}{(1-x)^2}\) in terms of \(\frac{1}{1-x}\text{.}\) Notice that

\begin{align*} \frac{1}{(1-x)^2} &= \diff{}{x}\left\{\frac{1}{1-x}\right\}\\ &= \diff{}{x} \left\{\sum_{n=0}^\infty x^n \right\}\\ &= \sum_{n=1}^\infty nx^{n-1} \qquad\text{by Theorem } \knowl{./knowl/thm_SRpsops.html}{\text{Teorema 1.13}} \end{align*}

Note that the \(n=0\) term has disappeared because, for \(n=0\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} \diff{}{x} x^n = \diff{}{x} x^0 = \diff{}{x} 1= 0 \end{equation*}

Also note that the radius of convergence of this series is one. We can see this via Theorem   Teorema 1.13. That theorem tells us that the radius of convergence of a power series is not changed by differentiation — and since \(\sum_{n=0}^\infty x^n\) has radius of convergence one, so too does its derivative.
