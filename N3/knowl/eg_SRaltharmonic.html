Exemplo 1.15. Convergence of the alternating harmonic series.

We have already seen, in Example Exemplo 1.6, that the harmonic series \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty\frac{1}{n}\) diverges. On the other hand, the series \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty(-1)^{n-1}\frac{1}{n}\) converges by the alternating series test with \(A_n=\frac{1}{n}\text{.}\) Note that

  1. \(A_n=\frac{1}{n}\ge 0\) for all \(n\ge 1\text{,}\) so that \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty(-1)^{n-1}\frac{1}{n}\) really is an alternating series, and

  2. \(A_n=\frac{1}{n}\) decreases as \(n\) increases, and

  3. \(\lim\limits_{n\rightarrow\infty}A_n =\lim\limits_{n\rightarrow\infty}\frac{1}{n}=0\text{.}\)

so that all of the hypotheses of the alternating series test, i.e. of Theorem Teorema 1.14, are satisfied. We shall see, in Example [cross-reference to target(s) "eg_SRpsrepC" missing or not unique], that

\begin{align*} \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n-1}}{n} &= \log 2. \end{align*}
