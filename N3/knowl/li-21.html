On the other hand, the series \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty\frac{1}{n^{1.000001}}\) has \(p = 1.000001 \gt 1\) and so converges. This time as we add more and more terms of this series together, the terms we add, namely \(\frac{1}{n^{1.000001}}\text{,}\) tend to zero (just) fast enough that the full sum is finite. Mind you, for this example, the convergence takes place very slowly â€” you have to take a huge number of terms to get a decent approximation to the full sum. If we approximate \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty\frac{1}{n^{1.000001}}\) by the truncated series \(\sum_{n=1}^N\frac{1}{n^{1.000001}}\text{,}\) we make an error of at most
\begin{align*} \int_N^\infty \frac{dx}{x^{1.000001}} & = \lim_{R\rightarrow\infty} \int_N^R \frac{dx}{x^{1.000001}}\\ & = \lim_{R\rightarrow\infty}- \frac{1}{0.000001} \Big[\frac{1}{R^{0.000001}}-\frac{1}{N^{0.000001}}\Big]\\ & =\frac{10^6}{N^{0.000001}} \end{align*}
This does tend to zero as \(N\rightarrow\infty\text{,}\) but really slowly.
